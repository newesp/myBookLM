# Impersonating Jed McKenna

A deceptively light chapter — a lunch with his sister — that reveals one of
the less-discussed realities of truth-realization: the quiet impossibility
of returning to old relationships. The chapter opens with Bernadette
Roberts's question that frames the whole piece:

> "With the clear certitude of the self's disappearance, there automatically
> arose the question of what had fallen away — what was the self? What,
> exactly had it been? Then too, there was the all-important question:
> what remained in its absence?"

## The Setup

Summer 2001, lower Manhattan. Jed is having lunch with his sister, whom he
hasn't seen in years. She's read an advance copy of *Damnedest*. She is,
in his loving description: *"a successful executive, wife, mother,
Republican, tennis nut, Christian-ish, and all-round productive member of
society."* She is not the book's target audience.

The line running through his mind throughout: **No man is a prophet in his
own country.**

## The Core Observation

The entire chapter hinges on one passage:

> "The problem is that these people, my family, are all related to my
> shell, and I'm not. They're looking at the outer Jed McKenna and assuming
> an inner Jed McKenna. I'm inside Jed McKenna looking out and I can't
> really remember what he's supposed to do or say. It's all fakery. I'm an
> actor playing a role for which I feel no connection and have no
> motivation."

And the deeper layer he brackets aside:

> "(The whole thing is further entangled by the fact that there's no 'I'
> inhabiting my shell, just a fading echo, but let's not go down that road
> just now.)"

## The Nature of Post-Awakening Identity

Jed is clear that this is not confusion — it is the opposite:

> "I possess not the least shred of doubt about who and what I am. The
> tricky thing is that who and what I am is not related to this pretty,
> professional, salad-eating woman across from me."

The past they share is still nominally his past, but:

> "The past I share with this person is about as real and important to me
> as if I'd read it in a brochure."

This is not coldness. It is accuracy. The person who lived that childhood
is no longer here. There is fondness — he says he'd be saddened if she
died — but the *relationship* in any meaningful sense no longer exists.

## Why Share This?

Jed himself raises the question:

> "Okay, so why am I telling you this?
> Because that's what I do. I try to hold this enlightenment thing up for
> display and this seems like an interesting aspect of the whole deal.
> **How do you relate to the people who were most important to you before
> awakening from the dream of the segregated self?**"

This is the pedagogical purpose: to put a real question in front of the
reader. It's easy to imagine enlightenment as sitting in bliss on a
mountain. It's harder to imagine it as having lunch with your sister and
realizing you've forgotten your lines.

## The Astrologer Joke

A telling moment. Jed makes a flippant joke: *"My astrologers told me it
was a good time to get away..."* His sister stops chewing. She takes him
seriously. He tries to extricate himself with more jokes — *"I have
dozens of astrologers. Can't swing a dead cat without hitting someone
who's doing my chart"* — but she can't tell the humor from the reality.

The chasm is not about astrologers. It's this:

> "The truth is that I don't possess any mechanism that would allow me to
> be curious or concerned about the future, but saying that doesn't make
> for breezy conversation."

This is an important detail about the awakened state. It's not that Jed
plans his life differently. It's that the *mechanism itself* — the
forward-leaning anxiety-curiosity about what might happen — is gone.
There is nothing to say about it that doesn't sound either like mystical
posturing or like indifference.

## The Impersonation Failure

Midway through the lunch, Jed names what's actually happening:

> "In accepting this lunch engagement, my hope was that I could slip back
> into my old persona enough to manage a civil meal. That was too hopeful.
> **I can no longer impersonate myself** and I am simply unable to
> formulate a reply to anything she has to say; I've forgotten my lines."

The phrase "impersonate myself" is the chapter's whole title, and it names
a specific post-awakening reality: the old self was a role that could, with
effort, be performed. Now the actor who played that role is gone. Even
mediocre social competence in the old context requires an actor who
remembers the lines.

## "Power Corrupts" — The Sister's Concern

She lowers her voice and warns him about spiritual teachers who abuse
their power for "unsavory purposes." It's an earnest, big-sisterly
concern.

Jed could explain to her that what he now is doesn't relate in the way she
imagines. But:

> "But why say it? To satisfy myself? It wouldn't. To inform her? It
> wouldn't."

Instead, he breaks the tension with elaborate comedic absurdity — the
Darth Vader boom-box entrance, the robes, Shri Shri Shri Shri Jed, speaking
in the first person plural. She laughs. The ice breaks. They part with
genuine fondness.

## The Closing Note

> "I doubt I'll ever see her again, but I'm happy knowing she's still in
> the world."

This single sentence is the teaching. There is real affection. There is
no expectation of continued relationship. These coexist without conflict.
This is what love looks like after no-self — unclouded by need, regret,
nostalgia, or obligation. Not less love. A different kind.

## What This Chapter Teaches About Awakening

1. **The awakened person is not a better version of the old person.** They
   are categorically other. Old relationships depended on an old person
   who is not there anymore.

2. **"Imposter syndrome" has a literal meaning here.** The awakened person
   who tries to resume their old role is not performing insincerely — they
   literally cannot find the person who used to live that role.

3. **The loss of forward-leaning concern.** No curiosity about the future,
   no anxiety about outcomes, no mechanism for worry. This is not stoic
   discipline. It's structural absence.

4. **Affection without attachment.** He loves his sister. He doesn't need
   to see her again. Both are true. Neither compromises the other.

5. **Humor is the bridge.** When the truth can't be communicated, jokes
   can. Not as deception — as a way of honoring the conversation without
   forcing a disclosure that wouldn't land.

## How to Apply This Teaching

**When users worry about losing their relationships during or after awakening:**
Don't reassure them that nothing will change. The relationships *will*
change, because one of the parties is changing. But the change is not
necessarily loss. What dies is the role-based transaction. What can
remain is the simpler fondness of wanting someone to still be in the world.

**When users feel like imposters in their old social contexts:**
This may not be pathology. If the inner person has genuinely shifted and
the outer role has not, "imposter" may simply be the correct descriptor.
The question is not how to feel less like an imposter but whether to
continue playing the role.

**When users feel guilty about emotional distance from family:**
Name the Bernadette Roberts question: *what fell away, and what remains?*
The loss of the old mechanism of connection is not the loss of love. But
it may be the loss of the performance that was being mistaken for love.

**When asked "can enlightened people have normal relationships?":**
The honest answer from this chapter: not the way the old relationships
were. Relationships persist in transformed form. Affection is possible,
perhaps deeper. The performance of being-the-old-person is not.
