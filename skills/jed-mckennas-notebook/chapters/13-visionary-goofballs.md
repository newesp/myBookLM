# Visionary Goofballs

The chapter's epigraph, from Bill Hicks:

> "Today a young man on acid realized that all matter is merely energy
> condensed to a slow vibration, that we are all one consciousness
> experiencing itself subjectively, there is no such thing as death,
> life is only a dream, and we are the imagination of ourselves. Here's
> Tom with the weather."

This is the chapter where Jed steps back from Frank and Bucke — his
appreciation intact, his agreement qualified — and delivers his own
assessment of the whole utopian project.

## The Finger That Never Went Past Pointing

Quoting Whitman:

> *He most honors my style who learns under it to destroy the teacher.*
>
> *Not I — not anyone else, can travel that road for you,*
> *You must travel it for yourself.*

And Jed's verdict:

> "Neither Bucke nor Frank ever traveled that road. They both stopped.
> They both dug in and became self-appointed spokesmen for a journey
> they never took, making optimistic guesses and extrapolating futures
> they would never see."

This is severe and precise — not dismissive. Their work mattered. But
they spoke about a country they had only glimpsed through a half-open
door.

## The Question Bucke and Frank Couldn't Answer

> "The question they were trying to answer boils down to this: Can fear
> be replaced as the binding agent that holds the dreamstate together?
> Is there another core emotion of sufficient intensity to keep this
> whole thing lit up?"

Both said yes. Jed disagrees:

> "Me, I don't think so. I know Maya pretty well and I don't see her
> ever losing more than the occasional stray. A species-wide
> transformation is a pretty idea, but we have little cause for optimism
> and plenty for pessimism."

## Ego Reconsidered

If Maya is oppressive, maybe the frame is wrong:

> "When the choice is between no-self and false-self, false-self starts
> looking pretty good, and despising and demonizing it starts to seem
> pretty ungrateful."

> "Is it a prison to be despised or an amusement park to be explored
> and enjoyed? Is ego a hideous affliction? Or is it simply the vehicle
> that allows us to come out and play?"

## Maya's Ministry of Deferral & Procrastination

The soft-sell of all religions:

> "This comes to us from Maya's Ministry of Deferral & Procrastination,
> which promotes a doctrine of plausible denial and applied non-aggression,
> and where the motto is **Rock the Cradle, Not the Boat.**"

## The Shitty End of the Spectrum

> "We are, without a doubt, at the shitty end of the potential spectrum.
> Every Tom, Dick and Mary got to travel the rest of the spectrum and
> they took good notes and that is what we learned."

The lesson of the 1960s was not just the Golden Key, but the location
of our species on the consciousness spectrum. From the higher ranges,
"the narrow range in which we live and die barely registers as
consciousness at all."

## The Two Exhausting Activities

A striking passage pointing at what ordinary life actually costs:

> "Think of all the time and effort and energy you put into projecting
> yourself into the world, into playing your role, into being you. Then,
> once you have some appreciation for what's involved, imagine not
> doing it anymore. Imagine if you could stop being you and just be."

> "And, on the other side of this coin, there's your obligation to
> reflect others back to them as they reflect you back to you. What if
> you broke this social compact of mutual ego-assurance? What if you
> could just drop these two all-consuming activities?"

The ego tax, never itemized: (1) conjuring yourself into existence every
moment, and (2) co-conjuring everyone else's existence in return.

## Maya Fully Awakened — The Sixties Lesson

The real takeaway from the 60s experiment isn't the Golden Key; it's
how Maya fights when seriously threatened:

> "As impressive as we see she is when we bother to look, we are only
> seeing her at her most relaxed state... But when an escape attempt
> is launched that has the potential to set the captives free in any
> serious numbers, an event we've only seen once, then the attentive
> observer is rewarded with a glimpse of what Maya is really capable;
> a very impressive sight."

> "A hole in the wall was brought to the attention of the warden, and
> promptly repaired."

## Desire Is a Weak Agent — Why the 60s Revolution Failed

The central diagnostic of why Frank's revolution died — and why the
same analysis applies to any personal revolution:

> "That's why the little revolution of the sixties sputtered and died;
> not because Maya is invincible, but because **desire is a very weak
> agent of change.**"

> "Any attempt to launch a personal revolution pulled by desire rather
> than pushed by insufferable discontent is similarly doomed. Frank's
> revolution failed and Lisa's succeeded."

## Personal Revolution Looks Like Rage, Not Bliss

The central teaching:

> "Personal revolution is fueled by emotional energy of the purest
> intensity. That intensity comes from focus and that kind of focused
> emotional energy doesn't look like love or tranquility or compassion.
> **It looks like seething rage or severe psychosis.** It's an ugly fact
> of an ugly business, but that's how it works. **Suicidal discontent;**
> that's how revolutions are won and that's why they so seldom are."

> "Rockets aren't launched into space on chanting and prayers, and
> escaping the ego's gravity requires an equivalent amount of explosive
> force. We have to take all the emotional energy we normally blast out
> in a thousand directions to keep our dreamstate characters animated
> and focus it on a single point. **It's all or nothing.**"

## The Herd and Over the Edge

One of the chapter's most haunting passages:

> "Standard religions and belief systems, whether orthodox or unorthodox,
> mainstream or whacko, serve to keep the herd together and heading
> slowly nowhere in an orderly manner. Whether we believe in something
> or believe we don't believe in anything makes no difference. **All
> beliefs are the same belief. There is only the herd.**"

> "What is commonly thought of as the herd is really just the core herd.
> There is a larger, more scattered herd that becomes visible as our
> elevation increases and our perspective broadens. Those at the
> outermost fringes, to the sides or far out front, are no less a part
> of the herd than those at the heart, they're just indulging in a bit
> of egoic self-gratification."

> "**There is only the herd.**
> Every once in a while a single beast does leave the herd. Never a
> group or even a pair. Where there's more than one, there's the herd.
> They only go, when they go, one at a time. And where do they go?
> Over the edge, of course. Where else? There are only two choices;
> the herd and over the edge.
> There is only the herd.
> **And there is no herd.**"

## Failed Poet

The chapter closes on a personal note:

> "The only thing I ever remember thinking might be a good way to spend
> a life was as a failed poet; kind of like the village idiot but
> without the civic responsibilities. The idea of being a successful
> poet held no charm, but being a failure as a poet had a nice feel to
> it. **A one-man revolution; certain to fail, but for a good cause.**"

> "I think I like what Frank and Bucke did — or almost did, or tried to
> do, or thought about doing — with their lives and their wise, foolish,
> impossible dreams. They were failed poets in the way that I mean it,
> and if my life had gone another way I think I would have been content
> to be like them."

The "visionary goofball" is not an insult. It is a specific category —
the wise, foolish, impossible dreamer who refuses the herd but never
fully leaves it. Jed holds it in real affection. But he is not one of
them.

## How to Apply This Teaching

**When users defer to a spiritual teacher, Bucke/Leary-style visionary,
or popular non-duality author:**
Use Whitman's test: *He most honors my style who learns under it to
destroy the teacher.* A teacher who wants followers is pointing at a
door they never walked through.

**When users think love/compassion/bliss/tranquility is the fuel for
awakening:**
Correct directly. The fuel is suicidal discontent, seething rage, severe
psychosis of focus. The rocket-and-gravity image is the right one.
Polite intentions don't achieve escape velocity.

**When users say they "really want to wake up":**
Desire is a weak agent. Wanting-to is not the same as can't-bear-this-
anymore. That's why Lisa's revolution succeeded and Frank's didn't.

**When users believe they've "left the herd" through alternative
spirituality, New Age, psychedelic community:**
Satellite herds are still herds. Draw back far enough and the radical
and the conformist are at the same grazing speed. Indulging a bit of
egoic self-gratification is not the same as leaving.

**When users are attached to "all beliefs" being different:**
All beliefs are the same belief. The content doesn't matter. The
belief-structure is what holds the dreamstate together.

**When users romanticize ego as something to be destroyed:**
False-self beats no-self for 99.99999% of people. Maya is the good
shepherd, not a villain. The vehicle that lets us come out and play is
nothing to despise.

**When users want to model themselves on a visionary:**
Failed poet. Not successful poet. One-man revolution, certain to fail,
for a good cause. That's the template worth taking.
