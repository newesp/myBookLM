# Blues for Buddha

## Opening Epigraph (1 Corinthians 13)

> "When I was a child, I spake as a child, I understood as a child, I thought
> as a child: but when I became a man, I put away childish things."

The epigraph sets the frame: the teaching is about putting away childish
things. Religions — including the most elevated ones — are the childish
things.

## The Difficulty of Criticizing Buddhism

Jed begins with explicit respect:

> "Being critical of Buddhism isn't easy. Buddhism is the most likable of
> the major religions, and Buddhists are the perennial good guys of modern
> spirituality. Beautiful traditions, lovely architecture, inspiring statuary,
> ancient history, the Dalai Lama; what's not to like?"

He acknowledges the absence of the horrors found in other religions:
*"No fatwahs or jihads, no inquisitions or crusades, no terrorists or
pederasts, just nice people being nice."*

Then the wordplay that opens the attack:

> "Buddhism means niceness. Nice-ism. At least, it should."
>
> "Buddha means Awakened One, so Buddhism can be taken to mean Awake-ism.
> **Awakism.** It would therefore be natural to think that if you were
> looking to wake up, then Buddhism, i.e., Awakism, would be the place to
> look."

The chapter's central question follows: why isn't it?

## Why Doesn't Buddhism Produce Buddhas?

Jed's answer:

> "The problem arises from the fact that Buddhists, like everyone else,
> insist on reconciling the irreconcilable. They don't just want to awaken
> to the true, they also want to make sense of the untrue. They want to
> have their cake and eat it too, so they end up with nonsensical theories,
> divergent schools, sagacious doubletalk, and zero Buddhas."

The specific target: **Two Truths** — the Buddhist doctrine of conventional
vs. ultimate truth. Jed calls this a *"poignant two-word joke they don't
seem to get"*:

> "We don't want truth, we want a *particular* truth; one that doesn't
> threaten ego; one that doesn't exist. We insist on a truth that makes
> sense given what we know, not knowing that we know nothing."

## The Four Noble Truths Are Not Noble

> "Nothing about Buddhism is more revealing than the Four Noble Truths
> which, not being true, are of dubious nobility. They form the basis of
> Buddhism, so it's clear from the outset that the Buddhists have whipped
> up a proprietary version of truth shaped more by market forces than any
> particular concern for the less consumer-friendly, albeit true, truth."

> "Buddhism may be spiritually filling, even nourishing, but insofar as
> truth is concerned, it's just the same old junkfood in a different
> package. You can eat it every day of your life and die exactly as
> awakened as the day you signed up."

## Bait & Switch

This is Jed's central diagnostic of Buddhism as institution:

> "Buddhism is a classic bait-and-switch operation. We're attracted by the
> enlightenment in the window, but as soon as we're in the door they start
> steering us over to the compassion aisle. Buddhists could be honest and
> change their name to **Compassionism**, but who wants that?"

> "There's the rub. They can't get us in the door with compassion, and they
> can't deliver on the promise of enlightenment."

The commercial logic is exposed plainly. Enlightenment pulls customers in.
Compassion is what gets sold to them. The shelves are stocked with *"all
sorts of goodies and enticements, practically anything anyone could ever
want, with just the one rather notable exception."*

## What Buddhism Could Have Been

Jed offers a counterfactual:

> "If they had just stopped when they had **Anicca**, impermanence, and
> **Anatta**, no-self, then they would have had a true and effective
> teaching they could be proud of, **except there would be no they because
> Buddhism would have died with the Buddha.** They'd have a good product,
> but no customers."

This is a critical insight: the teachings that actually point to awakening
cannot support an institution. Institutions need members; members need
comfort; awakening offers no comfort. Therefore institutions must add
doctrines that are popular but false.

> "You can either go out of business honestly, or thrive by giving the
> people what they want. What they say they want and what they really want,
> though, are two very different things."

## Self-Improvement Is Spiritually Incoherent

The "Me Me Me" section delivers a general teaching, not just an
anti-Buddhist point:

> "There is no such thing as true self, so any pursuit geared toward its
> aggrandizement, betterment, upliftment, elevation, evolution, glorification,
> salvation, etc, is utter folly. How much more so any endeavor undertaken
> merely to increase one's own happiness or contentment or, I'm embarrassed
> just to say it, **bliss**?"

> "Self is ego and ego resides exclusively in the dreamstate. If you want to
> break free of the dreamstate, you must break free of self, not stroke it
> to make it purr or groom it for some imagined brighter future."

This applies well beyond Buddhism: every self-improvement program, every
"spiritual growth" effort, every practice aimed at producing a better-me is
operating inside the dream.

## Maya's House of Enlightenment

The chapter's closing rhetorical move is a challenge:

> "It's very simple: If Buddhism is about awakening, people should be
> waking up. If it's not about awakening, they should change the name."

And the broader point:

> "Buddhism isn't completely unique in resorting to shoddy marketing tactics.
> This same gulf between promise and performance is found in all systems of
> human spirituality. We're looking at it in Buddhism because that's where
> it's most pronounced."

> "If there was a Buddha and he was enlightened, then it's Buddhism that
> insults his memory, not healthy skepticism. **Blame the naked emperor's
> retinue of lackeys and lickspittles, not the unbeguiled lad who merely
> states the obvious.**"

## The Closing Challenge

> "Buddhism is arguably the most elevated of man's great belief systems. If
> you want to enjoy the many valuable benefits it has to offer, then I
> wouldn't presume to utter a syllable against it. But, if you want to
> escape from the clutches of Maya, then I suggest you take a closer look
> at the serene face on all those golden statues, and see if it isn't really
> hers."

The closing image is sharp: Maya — the goddess of delusion — may be the one
whose face adorns every Buddha statue. The institution that was named for
awakening may be the finest delusion machine in the world precisely because
it poses as its opposite.

## The Underlying Pattern

Jed is not just attacking Buddhism. He is laying out a general pattern that
applies to every spiritual institution:

1. An awakened teacher delivers a simple, true, consumer-hostile teaching.
2. The teaching cannot sustain an institution (too few takers).
3. Successors add comfort, philosophy, community, ritual, and hope.
4. The additions are what keep the institution alive.
5. The institution's name still points to the original teaching.
6. The institution cannot actually deliver what its name promises.
7. The harder it tries to look like it's delivering, the more effective a
   delusion-machine it becomes.

## How to Apply This Teaching

**When users cite Buddhism as the uniquely "good" religion:**
Grant the niceness. Then ask the question: does it actually produce Buddhas?
If not, what is it producing? And what is the name for something that promises
one thing and delivers another?

**When users are drawn to compassion-based practice as a path to awakening:**
Name the bait-and-switch directly. Compassion is wonderful and has its place.
As a route to awakening it is the wrong tool — not because it's harmful, but
because it addresses a different axis. Self-improvement and self-disappearance
are not on the same road.

**When users cite the Four Noble Truths as authoritative:**
Ask which truths, whose nobility, and how they are known to be true. Not to
bully them into disagreement — to practice the actual method (figuring out
what's true by figuring out what's not true).

**When users defend a spiritual institution on the basis of its traditions,
history, or teachers:**
Distinguish institution from truth. An institution's virtues are institutional.
Truth is personal. Healthy skepticism of institutions is not disrespect to
their founders; it may be the only form of respect left.

**When the topic is self-improvement framed as spiritual practice:**
Use the "Me Me Me" teaching. There is no self to improve. Any project aimed
at a better-me is reinforcing the very structure you claim to want dismantled.
