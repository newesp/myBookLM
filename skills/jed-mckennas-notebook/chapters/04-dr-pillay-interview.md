# Dr. Pillay Interview

Four questions from Dr. Kriben Pillay, editor and founder of *The Noumenon
Journal*. Each answer is unusually concentrated — this is a dense chapter
despite its brevity.

## Question 1 — On "Abiding Non-Dual Awareness"

**The framing**: McKenna explains why he uses that specific term and what's
wrong with "Spiritual Enlightenment" as a label.

Key distinctions he draws:

- **Non-Dual Awareness** — a reasonable portrayal of the state *from within
  the state*
- **Abiding** — the word that distinguishes it from fleeting experiences
  commonly mistaken for, or sold as, Spiritual Enlightenment

Then the real observation:

> "The worst term for Spiritual Enlightenment is Spiritual Enlightenment."

McKenna himself didn't realize until *several years after his awakening*
that what he had become was what people meant when they said "Spiritual
Enlightenment" — at least those who used the term to mean the absolute state
rather than Unity Consciousness.

### Unity Consciousness vs. Truth-Realization

> "The experience of Unity Consciousness certainly feels more like something
> you'd want to call Spiritual Enlightenment, but it's not lasting, so what
> is it? A sweet dream. The most wonderful thing we can experience, I'd
> agree, but not a permanent awakening from delusion."

**Unity Consciousness = a sweet (temporary) dream.**
**Truth-Realization = permanent awakening from delusion.**

### The Preferred Term

> "The best term might be **Truth-Realization**. The term Spiritual
> Enlightenment perpetuates the mist-enshrouded, mountain-top mystique,
> whereas Truth-Realization sounds natural, reasonable, and within reach.
> Spiritual Enlightenment is for the ultra-elite, but truth is everyone's
> business."

This is a crucial reframe. Anyone who places enlightenment on a mountaintop
reserved for the special few has already taken themselves out of the game.
Truth, on the other hand, is ordinary. Available. Everyone's business.

## Question 2 — On Using the Term "Abiding Non-Dual Awareness" in Spiritual Autolysis

Pillay raises a subtle issue: if an unenlightened seeker calls enlightenment
"Abiding Non-Dual Awareness" during their own Spiritual Autolysis, are they
not just importing jargon?

McKenna agrees. A term can be adequate *from within* but useless *from
without*. The label "Abiding Non-Dual Awareness" was meant to hold up the
daily, even *banal* reality of the awakened state as evidence — to say
"Look, this is it."

For the seeker's own work, he recommends different vocabulary:

> "For the purposes of trying to achieve this state, I think the terms
> **No-Self**, **Truth-Realization**, and **Non-Duality** are of more
> practical value."

The lesson: match the term to the function. Descriptions-of-the-state are
not the same as tools-for-the-inquiry.

## Question 3 — "Can You Recommend Other Authors?"

This is one of McKenna's most characteristic responses. He refuses to
answer:

> "I'm going to sidestep this question in order to provide a more pertinent
> answer. The goal of the genuine seeker is always to take the next step,
> to open the next door. Waking up is not a scholastic pursuit or a
> conceptual challenge. The ability to open the next door is the only thing
> that matters, and the key can come in any package; a book, a stubbed toe,
> an advertising jingle, a leaf of grass."

Then the payoff:

> "If your intent is in place, then the universe will act as your librarian
> and you'll always have what you need when you need it."

The teaching: the seeker doesn't need a reading list. The seeker needs
**intent**. With intent, the right book finds you. Without intent, a
curated reading list is just another delaying mechanism.

## Question 4 — On U.G. Krishnamurti, Free Will, and Destiny

Pillay raises the big philosophical tension: U.G. Krishnamurti (and Ramesh
Balsekar, whom McKenna has quoted) argue that the individual can do nothing
about becoming enlightened — *because* it means the end of the individual.
But McKenna says enlightenment requires conscious intention. How is that
not a contradiction?

### McKenna's Response — Three Parts

**Part 1 — The free-will-vs-destiny debate itself is a dry hole:**

> "The debate of destiny and free will is a non-issue; a dry hole. It is
> based on false assumptions and false knowledge. Through clear-seeing,
> rather than belief, any such question can be transcended/destroyed, and
> the whole matter left behind. It's just another gate to get stuck behind
> for awhile and eventually pass beyond when you discover it was never
> really there."

> "Obviously, no answer is possible. **The trick is in seeing that the
> question too is not possible.**"

**Part 2 — Yes, conscious intention is required. Emphatically:**

> "Not only can it happen through conscious intention, **it can only happen
> through conscious intention.**"

> "To say that the individual can do nothing to break free of dualistic
> perception because it results in non-dualistic perception is nonsensical,
> like saying a living being can't step off a cliff because it results in
> death, or that a dreamer can't wake up because he's dreaming."

**Part 3 — Reconciling with U.G.:**

> "However, if U.G. Krishnamurti and others were saying that **self cannot
> achieve no-self**, then that's perfectly correct. The end of the one marks
> the beginning of the other. No one can have their cake and eat it too,
> despite what so many egotists and profiteers would have us believe."

The subtle point: the self cannot *arrive* at no-self as a continuing
entity. But the self is absolutely the thing that swings the sword that
destroys it. The dreamer cannot *remain a dreamer* while awake — but the
dreamer is the one who must wake up.

### The Paradox, and What to Do With It

> "Is it a paradox? Yes. Can it be explained so people will understand? No.
> Can people come see for themselves and understand it directly? Yes. How?
> Claw, scratch, bite, and burn. **Burn everything.**"

## The Master Key — Jed's One Piece of Advice

The chapter closes with the clearest distillation of McKenna's method:

> "Think for yourself and figure out what's true. You figure out what's true
> through a process of elimination, by figuring out what's not true. That's
> the master key; follow the truth. Ultimately, that's my only advice. The
> clock is ticking and you're completely on your own. Forget concepts,
> forget philosophy, forget spirituality, forget what anyone else says,
> don't try to dictate terms. Just think for yourself and figure out what's
> true."

And then, a rare moment of encouragement:

> **"You can do it."**

## How to Apply This Teaching

**When users get hung up on terminology:**
Offer the hierarchy: *Spiritual Enlightenment* = misleading label.
*Unity Consciousness* = wonderful but temporary. *Truth-Realization* /
*No-Self* / *Non-Duality* = precise and practical.

**When users ask for a reading list:**
Don't give one. The universe is the librarian. What's needed is intent,
not curriculum. Point them at their own next door, not a bookshelf.

**When users get stuck on free will vs. determinism:**
Name it a dry hole. Not because the question is trivial, but because
arguing either side depends on false premises. The question dissolves
when clearly seen rather than philosophically resolved.

**When users cite "I can't do anything to awaken" (U.G., Balsekar, neo-Advaita):**
Validate the half that's true: self cannot survive no-self. Then correct
the other half: self is nevertheless the sword. Saying "there's nothing
to do" is often ego's final refuge — the most elegant possible excuse to
not do the work.

**When users are ready to hear the method:**
Think for yourself. Figure out what's true by eliminating what's not true.
Forget concepts, philosophy, spirituality, and what anyone else says. The
clock is ticking. You're on your own. You can do it.
