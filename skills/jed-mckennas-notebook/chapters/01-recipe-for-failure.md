# Recipe for Failure

## The Setup

Jed is sitting with **Kamiel**, a student who has spent years immersed in the
Ramana Maharshi / Nisargadatta Maharaj / Ramesh Balsekar / Jean Klein
neo-Advaita tradition. Kamiel arrives with a bulging notebook of questions
and opens with: *"A lot of teachers say that the necessary first step in
awakening is dissatisfaction — a gnawing discontentment on the feeling level.
Is that what you mean when you talk about intent?"*

What follows is a deliberate trap, set so Kamiel (and the reader) can see
the critical distinction that "a lot of teachers" don't even suspect exists.

## The Office Analogy — Part One (The Trap)

Jed constructs an elaborate analogy to draw Kamiel into the standard
spiritual narrative:

> You're sitting in a hundred-story skyscraper office. Everything is perfect —
> fancy furniture, great views, respect, admiration. You're very satisfied.
> But one day, dissatisfaction creeps in. The curtains don't match the
> credenza. The carpet is a fiasco. You've outgrown this place.

Jed walks Kamiel through the "correct" spiritual response:
- Become serious about it
- Strip the place to the floorboards
- Hire the best decorator (the guru)
- Buy books, attend lectures
- Let yourself be transformed by the process
- Years of dedicated effort

Kamiel gets excited and starts to complete the pattern himself:

> "The master decorator represents the guru and the redecorating process
> represents the spiritual transformation we undergo when we truly begin
> to challenge our beliefs and seek higher knowledge."

He thinks he's just grasped "purity of intent."

Jed smiles and then delivers the punchline:

> **"Fuck no. That's what I mean by recipe for failure."**

## Why This Is Failure

Jed is clear about what he's just demonstrated:

> "That's the sort of pathetic, half-assed approach that is absolutely certain
> to keep you confined to your current state. That's the sort of approach
> that everyone takes, and that's why they fail."

And the killer observation about the entire spiritual industry:

> "The very people and institutions that are supposedly dedicated to waking
> us up are doing exactly the opposite. They are lulling us into a more
> comfortable sleep. That's what we really want and that's what they really
> provide."

## The Office Analogy — Part Two (The Reality)

Jed restarts the analogy. Same office, but this time the dissatisfaction
has a different source: **you smell smoke**.

> "Wake up and smell the coffin, Kamiel. The building has always been on fire,
> you were just repressing that knowledge until now."

The analogy escalates:
- The flames are outside the door
- The door bursts into flames — there is no exit
- You hate your office profoundly now
- Your dissatisfaction is *searing*
- Career, home, family — all reduced to complete irrelevance
- Beliefs and concepts disappear
- Even death becomes small
- You're completely alone — no Jesus, no Buddha, no Pope, no mama

The escape isn't Hollywood glass. You hurl yourself at the window, break
bones, crack your skull. Either:
- You perish in the hellish inferno, **or**
- You break through the unbreakable window — now there's just you, the
  raging fire, and a hundred-story plummet to the sidewalk.

**Burn or jump.** Those are the options.

## The Actual Definition of Purity of Intent

> "When you become so dissatisfied with your office that the hundred-story
> plummet and the sidewalk seem like the better option, so dissatisfied
> that you actually hurl yourself out the window, then you know the level
> of dissatisfaction necessary to awaken from delusion."

Kamiel finally understands: "I guess dissatisfaction isn't the right word."

Jed agrees: *"I call it purity of intent, but that doesn't really capture
it either."*

## The Moral

> "The price. Of truth. Is everything."

Delivered in three deliberate pieces. Not a rhetorical flourish — a literal
accounting. Everything you identify with must go. Career. Relationships.
Beliefs. Concepts. The spiritual identity itself. Even the thing that's
afraid to lose those things.

This is why there are extremely few awakened masters. Not because it's
intellectually difficult — because the price is unthinkable and almost
no one will pay it.

## The Neo-Advaita Aside

Within this chapter Jed also addresses the neo-Advaita scene directly:

> "Not-two is not exactly true, two is exactly not true, and therefore
> succinctly marks the endpoint of dualistic thought... Where nondual
> enthusiasts go astray is in trying to erect a philosophical structure
> atop this simple truth. Truth is always simple and never provides the
> basis for any philosophy."

> "You can't build a philosophy of This on a foundation of Not-This."

But he's not hostile to the tradition. Nonduality is a real and
impressive plateau; reaching it takes serious effort and the views
are rewarding. The error is mistaking the plateau for the summit,
or worse, building a philosophy-shaped shelter on it and refusing
to move on.

## How to Apply This Teaching

**When someone describes their spiritual journey as "growth," "expansion,"
"transformation," or "becoming more of who I really am":**
Gently point to this chapter. These are redecoration words. Real awakening
is described in destruction language — burning, falling, losing, dying.

**When someone asks how to know if their motivation is "pure enough":**
The test is not intensity of feeling. The test is: would you jump out the
window if that were the only option? If you're still weighing pros and cons,
you're still shopping for curtains.

**When someone is stuck in neo-Advaita intellectualism:**
Validate that the non-dual plateau is a real achievement, but that building
philosophy on top of "not-two" is precisely the error. The teaching points
past itself. Philosophy of This on foundation of Not-This is structurally
impossible.

**When to share "the price of truth is everything":**
Reserve this for moments when the person is genuinely facing the question
of what awakening costs. Delivered too early, it's a cool quote. Delivered
at the right moment, it ends the conversation — which is the point.
