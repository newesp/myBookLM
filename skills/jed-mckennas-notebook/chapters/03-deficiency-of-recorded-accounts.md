# The Deficiency of Recorded Accounts

This is a brief chapter — McKenna does not write it himself, he simply
reproduces the opening passage from Bernadette Roberts's *The Experience
of No-self: A Contemplative Journey*, using it as a framing device.

## The Quote

Bernadette Roberts, a former Carmelite nun who wrote from a Christian
contemplative tradition, opens her book with this:

> "This is the personal account of a two-year journey during which I
> experienced the falling away of everything I can call a self. It was a
> journey through an unknown passageway that led to a life so new and
> different that, despite forty years of varied contemplative experiences,
> I never suspected its existence. Because it was beyond my expectations,
> the experience of no-self remained incomprehensible in terms of any frame
> of reference known to me, and though I searched the libraries and
> bookstores I did not find there an explanation or an account of a similar
> journey which, at the time, would have been clarifying and most helpful.
> Owing then to the deficiency of recorded accounts, I have written these
> pages trusting that they may be of use to those who share the destiny of
> making this journey beyond the self."

## Why This Chapter Matters

McKenna's inclusion of Roberts's passage without commentary is itself the
statement. Several things are being said silently:

1. **He is not alone.** Roberts describes the same terrain — a two-year
   journey through the falling away of self. The specifics echo McKenna's
   own timeline.

2. **The literature fails.** Forty years of contemplative experience and a
   thorough search of libraries yielded nothing clarifying. The spiritual
   marketplace does not map this territory.

3. **This book is filling that gap.** Just as Roberts wrote her account to
   serve those who come after, McKenna is doing the same. The function of
   the Enlightenment Trilogy is exactly what Roberts describes — to fill
   the deficiency.

4. **Truth-realization is cross-traditional.** Roberts arrived via Catholic
   contemplation; McKenna via independent inquiry. Neither lineage, neither
   religion, neither technique — but the same territory.

5. **"The destiny of making this journey."** Roberts uses the word destiny.
   Not everyone who hears the call makes the journey, but those who do are
   not choosing — they are fulfilling something.

## How to Apply This Teaching

**When users feel alone in their inner experience:**
The quote is a direct comfort. Someone with forty years of serious practice
couldn't find a map either. Lack of available accounts is not evidence of
the journey's unreality — it is evidence of how few have made it and
returned to describe it.

**When users ask about cross-tradition validation:**
Roberts is Catholic, McKenna is not. Ramana was Hindu. The Buddha was
Buddhist. The fact that the descriptions converge despite the traditions
diverging is itself a pointer.

**When users want a second source to read:**
Bernadette Roberts, *The Experience of No-self: A Contemplative Journey*
is McKenna's implicit recommendation. He will not list other authors when
asked directly (he redirects to Spiritual Autolysis instead), but this
chapter places Roberts in front of the reader.
