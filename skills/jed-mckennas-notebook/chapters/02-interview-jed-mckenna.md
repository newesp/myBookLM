# Interview with Jed McKenna

A Q&A format interview following the publication of the first book. The range
is wide — definitions, personal matters, teacher-student dynamics, mysticism,
the "shift in consciousness" mythology. This is the single most concentrated
source of McKenna's framework in the Notebook.

## Opening Epigraph

> "The truth is that enlightenment is neither remote nor unattainable. It is
> closer than your skin and more immediate than your next breath. If we wonder
> why so few seem able to find that which can never be lost, we might recall
> the child who was looking in the light for a coin he dropped in the dark
> because 'the light is better over here.'"

The coin-in-the-dark image is central: seekers look where it's comfortable
to look, not where the thing actually is.

## What Is Enlightenment?

McKenna's answer is four words: **No-self. Abiding non-dual awareness.**

- **Absolute** — doesn't come in varieties or degrees
- **Not open to interpretation** — it's not a matter of perspective
- **Self-verifiable and completely available to reason** — no interpreter needed
- **That which cannot be simpler** — it cannot be further reduced

He explicitly refuses to describe it beyond these terms:

> "There's no explaining fire to someone who's never seen fire; no description
> can do justice to the direct experience of fire. I use terms like abiding
> non-dual awareness and no-self and truth-realization not because they capture
> it, but because they seem the least misleading."

## The Contradiction of "No-Self" Saying Anything

When pressed on the apparent contradiction — how can there be a "Jed McKenna"
writing books if the teaching is no-self? — his answer is:

> "True self is no-self and there's just no way to make it sound reasonable...
> It's like the gateless gate thing. It looks one way from there and another
> from here. All I can say is come here and see for yourself."

The logic: non-duality is verifiable by any reasonably able mind. Once you
verify that all is one, the fiction of a separate self cannot long endure.

## Intelligence vs. Intent

> Q: What's really required by way of intelligence?
> A: "Not much, as my own success attests. It all really comes back to intent.
> If the intent is in place, everything is in place. If the intent isn't in
> place, no amount of intelligence will make any difference."

**Logic is the sword. Intent is the will to use it.**
Mind and heart, both required.

## On Promotion, Marketing, and Caring

On why he refuses book signings, radio, media: the book succeeds or fails on
things other than marketing. His job was to write it competently. The
publisher's job is to produce it. How it fares is none of their concern.

> Q: Do you care if it succeeds or fails?
> A: "I'd be amused if it succeeds. I'd be relieved if it fails. I had an urge
> to express certain things and now I have and the urge is gone."

## Enlightenment Is Not the Pinnacle

This is a key reframe:

> "We think of enlightenment as the pinnacle, as the best possible thing, but
> it's not. There's something far better and it's the thing everyone who is
> interested in spiritual enlightenment is really after."

He doesn't name "the far better thing" here — but this foreshadows the
Human Adulthood teaching developed later in the trilogy. People seeking
enlightenment are actually seeking something else.

## The Seller/Buyer Dynamic

> "The seller/buyer dynamic is the principle on which the entire spiritual
> marketplace operates, but anyone who wants to make real progress must leave
> all that codependent, mass-delusion nonsense behind."

The major religions, "viewed objectively, are nothing more than childish fairy
tales unworthy of serious consideration." If you can't break free of the
consumer mindset, that's where your intellect should be focused — not on
"some guy who wrote some book."

## Why He Stopped Teaching

Not a decision — an observation:

> "It's not that I stopped teaching; it's that I reached the end of the
> learning process necessary to bring the book into being. Everything, it
> turns out, was about the book."

The dialogues with seekers were part of the book's becoming. When the book
was done, the teaching-thing was done with it. Teaching was a costume.

## On Teachers and Gurus

> "Seeking a teacher is just ego seeking a reprieve; a stay of execution.
> Giving oneself over to a teacher or a teaching or the Beloved Guru or
> whatever is all about staying asleep, not waking up."

> "Ego clings to a teacher like a drowning man clings to a log. The teacher
> is beloved the way the log is beloved. The teaching is sacred the way the
> log is sacred. The log is the savior, saving us from sinking into the cold
> black depths."

On students who awakened under his guidance:

> "I was just there for them. A midwife can hardly take credit for the child.
> A signpost can hardly take credit for someone's successful journey."

## Waving People Off

> Q: You seem to advise people to see it for what it really is, not so they
> can attain it, but so they can stop trying.
> A: "What it really is is so far removed from what many seekers are seeking
> that it's only humane to try to wave them off."

The vast majority are best served by being waved off. Only a tiny percentage
are genuinely in the proximity of awakening. For the rest, **unfulfilled
seeking is its own life theme** — the seeking, not the finding, is the point.

> "In the context of being a human being on planet earth, seeking truth makes
> sense and finding it doesn't."

## The Amusement Park Analogy

One of McKenna's most useful frames:

> "Cosmic Consciousness and Altered States and Universal Mind are the names
> of rides in this vast and fascinating dualistic amusement park. So are
> Poverty and Disease and Despair. Enlightenment, though, is not another
> ride. Enlightenment means leaving the park altogether."

> "But why leave the park? In the park you can be a saint or a yogi or a
> billionaire or a world leader or a warlord. Be good, be evil. Happiness,
> misery, bliss, agony, victory, defeat, it's all here. What's the big rush?"

Awakening is inevitable like dying — you'll get there eventually no matter
what. So enjoy the park while you're in it.

## The Spiritual Marketplace Sells What Doesn't Exist

> "In most cases, the enlightenment being bought and sold is not enlightenment
> at all, but a state of consciousness so crazy-ass wonderful that you'd have
> to be an idiot not to want it. So insidiously wonderful, in fact, that its
> radiance has blinded untold millions of seekers to the fact that it doesn't
> exist."

The core deception: a blissful, desirable state is marketed as enlightenment.
The actual enlightenment ("no-self, abiding non-dual awareness") is unwanted
by the very aspect of us that could want it, because that aspect won't
survive the arrival. **Day destroys night.**

## On "The Coming Shift in Consciousness"

> Q: Do you see a massive shift in consciousness taking place?
> A: "No. People who talk about an enlightened humanity are talking about a
> world where the lamb lies down with the lion... a world without conflict,
> without drama. But that would be very silly since the whole point of
> duality is division. The whole point of opposites is opposition. This is
> a drama, not a still-life."

On shift-sellers:

> "Anyone who says there's a shift coming and those who follow their teaching
> will ride the wave are in the sales business — more accurately, they're in
> the business of selling something that's not theirs to sell. They get asked
> why no one who buys ever receives the product, and this is a stock answer;
> we'll all make the big shift together. That's how they keep the dogs
> wagging their tails."

## Instant Enlightenment — The Bucke Confusion

McKenna draws a sharp line between **cosmic consciousness / mystical
experience** and **truth-realization**:

> "I've had mystical experiences myself and I wouldn't even try to express
> the wonder of them. But the idea that someone can awaken to no-self in a
> flash is simply absurd."

Truth-realization is a roughly two-year process, plus about ten more years
of integration. Anyone claiming a flash-awakening is describing something
real but different.

## Alexander and the Chicago Library (Purity of Intent)

Jed illustrates purity of intent through his student Alexander — who, six
months before, had a normal wife/kids/house/job — deciding he needed to
reach a library in Chicago. From Los Angeles. Winter. No money. He hitchhikes,
walks through his soles, inserts folded newspaper into his shoes, does odd
jobs for food and a jacket, sleeps in doorways, reads when he can.

> "If you were to use the word *hardship* to Alexander as he sat exhausted,
> cold and hungry in that doorway, he'd have just stared at you as if you
> were a yapping dog."

> "There is no hardship, there's only the next thing, the next step, the
> next battle. The point is always to get the next door unlocked. The bible
> says to pluck out your eye if it offends you. If Alexander's eye were
> preventing him from opening the next door, it wouldn't occur to him not to
> remove it. **That's the fuel that drives this journey.**"

## Infinity as a Koan

McKenna reveals his implicit practice:

> "Infinity is beautiful; it destroys everything it touches. It annihilates
> all concepts, all beliefs, all sense of self. No teacher, teaching, book or
> practice could ever be as effective as simply allowing the thought of
> infinity to slowly devour you."

> "Spiritual Autolysis invariably leads to infinity. I mean, where else?"

The Western version of *mu*. Not a technique — an acid.

## The Goofiness Teaching

> "The First Noble Truth isn't 'Life is suffering,' it's 'Life is goofy.'
> The goofiest people are the ones who take it the most seriously, and no
> one takes it more seriously than someone who wants to awaken from it."

This is important: serious seekers are often the goofiest because they've
mistaken the park for the problem.

## How to Apply This Teaching

**When users ask "what is enlightenment?":**
Give the four-word answer first. No-self. Abiding non-dual awareness.
Then explain why elaborating it further would be misleading.

**When users mention their guru, teacher, or lineage:**
Not with contempt, but with the "drowning man and the log" frame. The
teacher-relationship's function is often to keep ego afloat, not to drown it.
A midwife cannot take credit for the child.

**When users describe mystical experiences as evidence of awakening:**
Honor the experience as real and wonderful. Distinguish it sharply from
truth-realization. These are rides in the park, not exits from the park.

**When users cite "the coming shift" / "ascension" / "new consciousness":**
Point out the logical problem. Duality is drama. A drama without conflict
is not enlightenment — it's a still-life, and a boring one.

**When users describe their seeking as endless without finding:**
Offer the permission to stop. For most people, seeking is the life theme.
The seeking, not the finding, is providing what they need. That's fine.
There's no moral failure in staying in the park.

**When users show genuine purity of intent:**
Don't interfere with the next door. Alexander's journey is the pattern.
Whatever it costs — plucked eye, sold house, shredded reputation — that's
the fuel. Don't water it down.
