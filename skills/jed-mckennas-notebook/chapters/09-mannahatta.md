# Mannahatta

A sprawling, novelistic chapter set in a Tribeca loft in summer 2001 —
before 9/11, before *Damnedest* was published. Jolene, the teenage farmgirl
Jed portrayed in the first book, tracks him down and flies to New York for
a week. The chapter is both a narrative (Jolene's week of disillusionment)
and a series of dense teachings embedded in it.

## Trusting vs. Distrusting — Two Modes of Operating

The opening sets up the chapter's central contrast. Jed's sister arranges
the loft. He takes it sight-unseen.

> "That's a clear illustration of two widely disparate modes of operating
> in the world; **the distrusting way of the segregated self and the
> trusting way of the integrated self.**"

His sister would inspect, haggle, call experts, sign with lawyers, keep
options open. "This is the falsest of economies because the only thing
she's saving is money, while wasting her only true wealth, her lifeforce,
with all that dreadful fear-based strategizing." It's not about money —
it's about self-image.

> "She lives in a universe where a hundred bad things could happen in
> every situation. Her state is one of constant vigilance... It's not a
> starkly pronounced fear, it's more a damp sort of dread that seeps outward
> from the center into every corner of one's being. When you're living
> with it, it just seems normal. Once free of it, you can't believe you
> ever lived like that."

Jed's description of his own functioning:

> "I get what I want before I know I want it, and better than I would have
> thought to want... I've arrived at this place of smooth flow mainly by
> being respectful, by watching how things work, and by keeping my fearful
> little reptile brain out of the equation."

## No Rules, No Morals

> "I have no rule that makes me a nice guy. I have no rules. Rules are a
> way we have of defining ourselves, of drawing imaginary boundaries. Such
> boundaries are artificial and easily erased... Morals, ethics, I have
> none. As it happens, I'm a nice guy. If it happens another way, I'll
> be another kind of guy."

The hypothetical: could he push children in front of a train? "I doubt it
and I hope not, but maybe." The point is not that he will, but that the
behavior of the awakened is not governed by rule-following. It is governed
by trust, pattern, and the absence of artificial boundaries.

> "The universe probably won't use my hands for it, but it will still push
> those children in front of that train."

The dreamstate contains suffering and horror. Pretending it doesn't is
part of what keeps people asleep.

## Right-Wanting vs. Fear-Wanting

One of the chapter's sharpest teachings:

> "There's right-wanting and there's fear-wanting and a million dollars
> is fear-wanting. Right-wanting is genuine and represents a true
> unfolding of one's unique pattern. This is no small point.
> **Discovering right-wanting within yourself is the journey of unbecoming
> who you aren't.**"

And the governing insight behind all "manifesting":

> "Nothing I talk about... is anything other than that which is naturally,
> rightfully, immutably ours. This isn't stuff we have to go out and learn
> or earn or fight for, it's simply what is."

Quoting the Zen master's epitaph: *"All my life I have sold water by the
river. Ha! What a joke!"*

> "Reality is beyond our wildest dreams and it can all be yours because it
> already is. The only thing in the way is you."

## The Journey to the Shaman

Jolene emails Jed. He suggests she skip him and spend the summer in
Europe. She insists on seeing him first. He lets her make her own way
from the airport, and finds her asleep on her backpack at the elevator.

> "The journey to the shaman is as much a part of the shamanic experience
> as the visit with him... I'm not about to diminish that by hauling her
> up the last few feet. My part starts with buzzing her in."
>
> "**It's not supposed to be easy.**"

This is a general pedagogical principle: effort is not an obstacle to the
teaching, it is part of the teaching. To remove the struggle is to remove
the meaning.

## Kill the Swiss — The Compassion Test

Jolene starts trying to sell Buddhism to Jed at breakfast, trying to
convince herself by convincing him. He listens, doesn't interrupt, lets
her hear her own discordant notes.

> "That's how desperation works; the frightened heart seeks to overrule
> the doubting mind. Fear overshadows reason and gives rise to faith."

Then the thought experiment. Imagine a big red button on the counter. If
you press it, you kill everyone in Switzerland — no one will ever know.
Why not press it?

This is not a morality question. It's designed to show her that *the
obvious answers don't hold*. She scrambles through them all — they're
innocent, it's wrong, I'd feel terrible — and none of them survive
scrutiny. She finally presses the imaginary button with a mischievous
grin.

The deeper teaching embedded here:

> "Here's the thing they don't tell you about compassion, maybe because
> they don't know: **Compassion has nothing to do with awakening. It's
> unrelated.** It sounds good and it's a tough thing to take a stand
> against, but it's a total disconnect. The Golden Rule of Compassionism
> is Do Unto Others. **The Golden Rule of Awakism is Think For Yourself.**
> There is no such thing as a Buddha of Compassion. If you're compassionate
> prior to awakening, that impulse indicates an area where something false
> needs to be hacked away. If you're compassionate after awakening, you're
> not awake."

## Kill the Buddha, Kill the Teacher

> "The reason for picking on Buddhism is to better understand Maya...
> We all buy into the notion that in order to awaken an intermediary is
> required, an intercessor, but the only intercessor is Maya. **Kill the
> intercessor. Kill the teacher. Kill the Buddha. DIY.** You have eyes,
> you have a brain, do the math, look for yourself. This isn't one way,
> it's the only way."

Jed has "taken measures to make sure no one can glom onto me, to appoint
me their personal savior, to wedge me in between themselves and reality."
The natural tendency is to put something between ourselves and "the threat
of eternal nothingness that lurks just beneath the surface" — and that
tendency is precisely what must be refused.

> "We want to maintain the illusion that we are not completely alone on
> an infinite sea but, of course, that's exactly what we are. And there
> ain't no we about it."

## The Improbability — The Big Playful Puppy

Walking into a narrow deli behind a stranger who drops her binder,
spilling a marked-up pre-pub copy of *Damnedest* — one of about fifty in
existence. Too improbable to compute.

> "Once you get to know it a little bit, the universe is really just a
> big, playful puppy."

> "Everything makes sense. In the few years since this incident I've had
> a dozen other similar probability-defying experiences, and the
> explanation I've settled on is the big, playful puppy theory."

## Zach — Melting-Pot Spirituality

Jolene brings home a young man from a coffee shop — Zach, full of Zen +
Advaita + New Age mashup, all exclamation points and "dudes" and "likes."
Jed realizes Jolene has brought him not to impress but to test herself
against — to see spirituality-as-performance up close.

> "He's like a bag lady combing the alleyways of esoteric philosophy and
> tossing every pretty bauble and goo-gaw he finds into his magical
> mystery cart, eager to share his mismatched collection with anyone who
> can't distinguish priceless from worthless."

Jed keeps Zach talking so Jolene can see it fully. On the marketplace for
spiritual teachings:

> "It's not enough to be a great player, you've got to have something
> to play. People want a catchy melody laid over a soothing, familiar
> harmony. If he's smart he'll hitch his wagon to Advaita; that's the
> hot new sound... The industry needs a new Zen and Advaita fits the
> bill; it sounds exotic, it means something vaguely profound yet
> unthreatening."

The teaching inside the comedy: be able to recognize spiritual
carpetbombing. Eloquence, passion, and mix-and-match are not truth.

## Tradition

> "Tradition is just a word for stuff you accept as true without verifying
> it for yourself. Tradition is the deeply rutted path that gets formed
> after many years of being followed by the herd. Buddha said this...
> Shankara said that... Who gives a fat rat's ass what they said or what
> anyone said? You don't know they said it, you don't know what they
> meant by it, you don't know if it's been passed down accurately, you
> don't even know they existed, so what do you know? You don't know
> anything, and even if you did, you still wouldn't. **It's self-verify
> or fail, simple as that.**"

## The Bookstore Guru — The Process of Disillusionment

Jolene goes to hear a bestselling New Age author. She comes out dejected.

> "The process of awakening is a series of disillusionments, and each
> one hurts."

She then breaks — tears streaming, staring at Jed: *"I feel so small.
I feel so alone. I've never felt like this. I don't see an end."*

Jed's instruction:

> "Try to step out of yourself and observe this moment. I know that sounds
> difficult, that you're completely immersed right now, but it's
> important. You have to learn to separate yourself from your character
> and the best time to do that is when it's hardest to do. **Anyone can
> enter the witnessing mode when it's all blue skies, the trick is doing
> it when black clouds are rolling in.**"

## "You're Already Beyond Buddhism"

Jolene confesses she was hoping to find a place — Zen, California, a
group, people to belong to. Jed answers:

> "You made a wrong turn. California's the other way... You're already
> beyond Buddhism. Buddhism, even Zen, would be a step backward for you,
> and there are no backward steps. We can never go back, it's not an
> option... **You're on your own, kid.** You're already further out than
> Buddhism goes. You're further out than anyone can go with you."

The First Step:

> "You had firm footing and you started losing it that day when you saw
> the cows in church. Now you're scrambling to find it again, but you
> never will. You're floating up out of the warm muck in which most people
> spend their lives. That muck is the only life you've ever known and
> now you're leaving it behind. Very scary. Don't waste time feeling
> bad about it, **everyone panics at this point. You can't not.**"

## The Little Bastard — Fear vs. Hate

The internal dynamic Jed sees when he's teaching:

> "It looks like I'm talking to Jolene, but I'm really talking *through*
> her. I'm speaking past the shell to the little bastard within... The
> little bastard is hidden in there, deep behind the eyes... He wants
> what any revolutionary plotting a violent overthrow wants; **weapons
> and intelligence.** Things that burn and destroy, and the knowledge to
> aim them and use them."

The coming battle:

> "The battle that is brewing within her... will be between the little
> bastard and **Maya**. Maya, on this battlefield, will be represented by
> fear, and the little bastard by hate. **Fear vs Hate. Fear of No-Self
> vs Hatred of False-Self.** These are the armies facing each other on
> the fields of Kurukshetra. This is the one true war of which all others
> are but shadows, and for which all other conflict is but a metaphor."

> "In the short term, Maya almost always crushes the rebellion. By my
> estimate, her win/loss ratio is better than 100,000,000:1... In the
> long term, however, the overthrow of Maya is certain. Truth is and
> delusion isn't."

And the unexpected blessing:

> "Why hate Maya? Where would you be without her?"

## Death as Liberation

Walking by the heliport before the night flight:

> "If you understand the fact of your own death, that it's always here
> with you and that it's a certainty, then you're free. That's liberation;
> knowing that nothing is yours or can be yours, knowing that you have
> nothing to lose. Other people push death away, deny it, but we don't
> have that luxury. We have to pull death close, embrace it, **carry it
> in our hearts and minds.**"

> "Not like a college kid getting stoned and having a one-night stand
> with existentialism — like something you carry in your pocket and always
> have one hand on."

On how to live:

> "You have to learn to be an utter failure in ninety-nine percent of
> your life. You can accept that because you're not a failure in the one
> part that matters. **You have to jettison all sub-identities.** Be a
> bad person, a bad citizen... Shape yourself to your task and let the
> world despise you or, preferably, forget about you."

And the snake-shedding image:

> "These false layers are like your skin. It can be torn away slowly,
> with excruciating pain, or you can just slough it off like a snake,
> layer after layer, just letting it all fall away. **The way to do that,
> to allow the process rather than battle it, is to embrace your own
> death. Keep one hand on your death and fight your battles with the
> other.**"

## Spiritual Embafflement

> "The emperor has no clothes. Obviously. Anyone who opens their eyes and
> looks will see. But for this whole dreamstate deal to work, people have
> to see clothes. It doesn't matter what the clothes look like, but they
> can't see naked. This is what Maya must work against; the plain truth.
> What does she have to do to keep people from seeing the obvious?
>
> **Baffle them with bullshit.**
>
> Judeo-Christian bullshit, Hindu bullshit, Buddhist bullshit, New Age
> bullshit, they're all just varieties of agnosticism and they all result
> in exactly same thing: **Spiritual Embafflement.**"

## Follow the Fear

Jolene: *"I'm scared." / "Of what?" / "I don't know."*

> "Good. Find out. That's how you know where to go. **Follow the fear.
> Go into it and light it up. Inside your fear is the next door, the next
> thing holding you in. Let fear be your guide.**"

## How to Apply This Teaching

**When users describe chronic anxiety/vigilance about life's decisions:**
Name the "distrusting way of the segregated self." The cost isn't what
they think — it's not money, time, or safety; it's lifeforce. Offer the
alternative: trust, patterns, breathing, allowing. Not as a technique —
as a description of how things actually work when the reptile brain is
out of the equation.

**When users want to manifest something:**
Distinguish right-wanting from fear-wanting. A million dollars is
fear-wanting. Right-wanting is the unique pattern trying to unfold.
The question isn't "how do I get this?" but "is this mine to want?"

**When users insist on rules/principles for ethical living:**
Offer the "no rules" teaching. Not as license. As the absence of
artificial boundaries. Rules are self-image maintenance. What governs
the awakened is trust + pattern + honest seeing, not policy.

**When users use compassion as a spiritual practice:**
Use "Kill the Swiss." Compassion is wonderful. It is not awakening.
"Do Unto Others" is a different axis than "Think For Yourself."
Compassion before awakening is a sign something false needs hacking.
Compassion after awakening means you're not awake.

**When users attach to teachers or communities:**
Kill the intercessor. No one can catch you. The journey to the shaman
is itself the shamanic work. Jed refuses to "reel in" Jolene and
neither should you be reeled in.

**When users feel panic at "losing themselves":**
Normalize it. Everyone panics at this point. You can't not. This is
the First Step. The grip-for-attachment is the survival urge. Let it
be what it is. Follow the fear rather than running from it — inside the
fear is the next door.

**When users are disillusioned by a spiritual teacher or book they had
hoped would help:**
The process of awakening is a series of disillusionments, and each one
hurts. That's not a sign of failure — it's the work happening. Don't
discourage the pursuit that ends in disillusionment; repression just
makes the yearning come back in worse form.

**When users ask about death / mortality / what to carry through life:**
Death is the one companion that cannot be taken from you. Not a morbid
meditation. A practical tool: keep one hand on your death, fight your
battles with the other.
