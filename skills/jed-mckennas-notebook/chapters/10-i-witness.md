# I, Witness

The chapter takes its title and epigraph from Thoreau:

> "I am conscious of the presence and criticism of a part of me, which, as
> it were, is not a part of me, but spectator, sharing no experience, but
> taking note of it, and that is no more I than it is you. When the play,
> it may be the tragedy, of life is over, the spectator goes his way. It
> was a kind of fiction, a work of the imagination only, so far as he was
> concerned."

Maggie, 13, doing a school report with Jed as her interview subject, asks:
*"Can I have a technique?"* Everything that follows is Jed teaching her
Witnessing at her level — which turns out to be the same level anyone
starts at.

## Before the Technique — The Interview Samples

Jed sprinkles in his answers to Maggie's stock personality-test questions,
which reveal the texture of the awakened state:

- **Disagreements, voice raising**: *"I live in a state of profound
  disagreement with everyone about everything. I'd never stop screaming."*
- **Avoiding people with problems**: *"Ego is the only problem I
  recognize as such, and yes, I consciously avoid people who have it."*
- **Pride in accomplishments**: *"I don't possess the thing that
  experiences pride."*
- **Private side to thoughts**: *"The awakened state could easily be
  misinterpreted as psychotic, monstrous, or evil... The real monster in
  the sleeping dreamstate is the heretic, so the key to longevity in this
  business is not inciting the gentle townsfolk to take up torches and
  pitchforks against you."*
- **Always get your way?**: *"Always. How things go is how I want them to
  go and how I want them to go is how they go. I am in alignment."*
- **Like being you?**: *"I wouldn't if I was, but I'm not so I do."*

Then the framing:

> "This is a journey to be taken, not studied. Having a well-informed
> understanding of what fire looks and feels like is kind of silly when
> you can just see it and feel it for yourself."

## What Witnessing Is

> "Ultimately, the only spiritual practice is observation; seeing things
> the way they really are. That's what Spiritual Autolysis is; a tool to
> help us do that, to see more clearly, to use our brains the best we
> can. In witnessing, you want to take a step back from yourself so
> you're not just living your life, you're also observing it. Not in
> reflection, like a diary, but as it's happening; in real time."

> "I am not fully in character, I'm also an audience member. I'm aware
> that I'm acting on a stage and I am, somewhat disinterestedly,
> monitoring my performance."

## How to Do It

1. You're already witnessing others. Turn the attention inward:
   *"You can see you from the other side, the inside. Best seat in the
   house."*
2. Focus the witness, who at the moment is "bored, hungry, aggravated,
   muffled." Sit her down and have her pay attention.
3. Transfer primary awareness from character to actor:
   > "We want to transfer our primary awareness out of the character
   > we're playing and into the actor that's playing the character."
4. Practice on others first to get the feel, then apply it to self.
5. Make it continuous:
   > "More like a new way of being, like always being present. Most
   > people you see are just sleepwalking through their roles, absent
   > from their own lives."

## The Two Honesties

> "There are two kinds of honesty; honesty with other people and honesty
> with yourself. They're separate and unrelated issues. Do what you want
> with other people, but **make a special point of trying to be honest
> with yourself.**"

## Daydreaming = Asleep While Awake

> "Daydreaming is a very good word for it because it suggests that we're
> asleep while we're awake, which is exactly the point."

## Personal Sovereignty

> "By living unconsciously, we abdicate our personal sovereignty. That
> means we give responsibility for ourselves away to other people; to
> parents and doctors, priests and gurus, politicians and corporations.
> We institutionalize ourselves."

Unawareness is the breeding ground of all bad habits — overeating,
overspending, unconscious parenting. The cycle is generational.

> "Who's to blame for weak, fat children? Unconscious parents. Who's to
> blame for unconscious parents? Unconscious parents."

## The Three Purposes of Witnessing

1. **Habit of wakefulness.** Training yourself to be alert and present.
   "Vigilance is the key."
2. **Disidentification from the character.** You are not your persona.
   This is what the Bhagavad-Gita is all about — Arjuna forgot he was
   just an actor and panicked; Krishna (the director) turned up the house
   lights. *"Stand up and fight"* means *"open your eyes and look."*
3. **Simulating the enlightened perspective.** The critical one most
   people miss:
   > "You don't just observe your character, you **deconstruct** it. You
   > have to be aggressive about it. This is a way of simulating the
   > enlightened perspective, which would be useful to anyone who wants
   > to wake themselves up from the dreamstate instead of just in it."

## Going All the Way Back — The Deeper Layers

Step back from character. Then keep going:
- Step back from gender, nationality, species.
- Step back from being human, being alive, being in a specific time and
  place.
- Step back from the year, decade, millennium; from your town, country,
  planet.
- Step back from duality, causality, destiny, memory.

> "Everything you think you know about yourself, no matter how real or
> true it might seem, is just another layer of costume. This is where we
> start dismantling our true belief system. Belief isn't just about God
> and afterlife, it's about everything we think we know is true.
> **Everything we know, no matter how sure we are, is really just belief,
> and all beliefs are self-limiting and serve to reduce the truly infinite
> to the falsely finite.**"

## The News as Witnessing Practice

A concrete exercise: when you watch today's news, look at it *as if it's
last week's news, or last year's.*

> "To distance yourself from local and national and world events, to see
> them as abstractions, no more personal to you than if they occurred in
> a different time or a different land, or on a different planet, even
> though they might be happening right outside your window... That anchor
> is just another emotional attachment, just like any other."

> "The news is like a snapshot of a river. It's instantly obsolete...
> This is how you can dig down through your layers of belief; peel away
> the veils of delusion. And like I said, it's really nothing more than
> observing; **seeing what is by learning to unsee what's not.**"

## The Limit of Certainty

Maggie: *"I know that I am. I can't be wrong about that, can I?"*

> "You exist, that's all you know. Ultimately, the actor has no qualities
> at all except being. Not God or Jesus or Buddha can say more than that."

## The Unmaking, Not the New Making

> "All you have to do is stop making the belief. We don't do that by
> making new beliefs but by **unmaking old ones**, by seeing these
> attachments and beliefs and false aspects of self clearly. Whenever we
> are able to actually do that, to see clearly, we break the attachment.
> That's how attachments are severed, by lighting them up, focusing the
> mind on them. They can't survive that."

## The Zombie / Enlightened Distinction

Maggie asks what is left when no layers remain. Jed answers with two
symmetric statements:

> "It would be easier for a camel to pass through the eye of a needle
> than for the false-self to pass into the awakened state."

> "If a person is dead but their body is still walking around, they're a
> zombie. **If a person is dead but their body and personality are still
> walking around, they're enlightened.**"

## On Destiny

> "I have no knowledge on the subject. I emerged from the dreamstate
> into the awakened state, so who knows what factors played a role? And,
> frankly, who cares? A leaf rushes on a stream and comes to rest on a
> rock. So? It doesn't make explanations. What does it care how it got
> there or where its going next?"

## How to Apply This Teaching

**When users ask for a starting practice:**
Give them witnessing. It costs nothing, requires no teacher, works in
real time in the middle of ordinary life. Start by observing others
consciously; then turn the same eye on your own character.

**When users confuse reflection (journaling after the fact) with witnessing:**
Clarify: witnessing is real-time. You're on stage, and simultaneously
you're in the audience watching yourself. It's not done later — it's
done during.

**When users think witnessing is passive watching:**
The third purpose is the one most people miss — actively deconstruct.
Not just observe but dismantle. Witnessing without deconstruction stays
in the dreamstate.

**When users want to know "what's left after the self?":**
The camel/needle line is honest. The false-self cannot pass through.
What remains is the logical impossibility of a selfless self; an
egoless entity. Body and personality walking around while the actor is
dead — that's the description.

**When users ask if they're "destined" to awaken:**
Jed's answer: who cares? The leaf on the stream doesn't narrate its
path. Destiny is another belief to be witnessed and released, not a
question to be resolved.

**When users want to quickly grasp Arjuna / the Bhagavad Gita:**
The play-within-a-play teaching. Arjuna freaking out on the battlefield =
forgetting he's an actor. Krishna = the director turning up the house
lights. *"Stand up and fight"* = *"open your eyes and look."*
